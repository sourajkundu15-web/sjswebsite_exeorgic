Project 1: Smart House Hologram

Participants: Rishi Talukdar (8C), Bipprodeep Modak (8C), Annirudha Deb (8C)

Description: The Smart House & Hologram project integrates security, automation, and futuristic display technology to enhance modern living. An IR sensor detects intruders, triggering an alarm and light for safety. A raindrop sensor protects clothes by automatically pulling them in with the help of a servo motor when rain is detected. Adding a unique touch, a hologram module projects a 3D image, showcasing advanced visualization technology. Together, these features create a smart, secure, and innovative home system that blends practicality with cutting-edge design.

Project 2: Remote Control Car with RADAR

Participants: Rayan Das (8C), Adrij Modak (8C), Shibom Chakraborty (8C), Aditya Saha (8C), Samriddh Makal (8C), Alipa (8C), Shrestha (8C)

Description: The Remote Control Car with RADAR is a compact reconnaissance vehicle built for remote surveillance and mapping; it is wirelessly controlled (ESP8266) and driven by DC motors through an L298N motor driver. A 180° servo-mounted sensor assembly acts as a RADAR-like scanner (ultrasonic) to sweep the surroundings while an onboard camera streams live video for real-time visualization and mapping. Ultrasonic sensing, along with buzzer and LED alerts, provides obstacle detection and local warnings; an LCD and potentiometer offer optional local display and control. Modular and portable, this platform combines robotics, sensing, and wireless connectivity to perform remote inspection, surveying, and situational awareness tasks (Components: Arduino, ESP8266, L298N, DC motors, servo, ultrasonic sensor, camera, buzzer, LEDs, breadboard, LCD, potentiometer).

Project 3: Toll Signal

Participants: Shreyash Shaw (8C), Aditya Soni (8C), Siddharth Tomey (8C), Aarav Gupta (8C), Tanishq Sharma (8C), Aritra Saha (8C)

Description: The Toll Signal project demonstrates an automated toll gate system controlled by Arduino. When the traffic signal turns red, the Arduino triggers a servo motor to rotate and close the toll barrier, effectively stopping vehicles. This setup simulates real-world toll management by integrating traffic lights with barrier control, making it a useful model for smart transportation systems. The servo ensures smooth gate operation, while the Arduino provides precise timing and logic control.

Project 4: Arduino UNO Human Following Robot

Participants: Dheer Chaterjee (8B), Ayan Dasgupta (8B), Kuldeep Sen (8B), Arav Singh (8B), Arthav Jaiswal (8B)

Description: The Arduino UNO Human Following Robot is an interactive robot designed to track and follow human hand movements. Built around an Arduino UNO board with an L293D motor shield as its control unit, the system uses an ultrasonic sensor and two infrared sensors to detect the position and movement of a human hand. These signals are processed to control four TT gear motors, enabling the robot to move forward, backward, left, or right in sync with the hand’s motion. With its programmed responsiveness, the robot demonstrates real-time human–machine interaction, making it an engaging project that combines robotics, sensors, and automation.

Project 5: Rain water harvesting with Hydro Electricity

Participants: Ilakshmita Roy (8A), Annaya Rai (8A), Kanak Damari (8A), Presta Rajak (8A), Tanvi Ghosh (8A)

Description: This project combines water conservation with renewable energy generation by collecting rainwater runoff, typically from rooftops, and channeling it to a micro-hydro turbine. It not only conserves water but also provides a sustainable source of clean energy, making it an eco-friendly and efficient dual-purpose solution.

Project 6: The Magnetic Car

Participants: Ayush Bose (8A), Arnab Sahoo (8A), Utso Sarkakr (8A)

Description: This project uses magnetism to control the movement of a car. By sliding magnets along the x-axis and y-axis, the direction and motion of the car are controlled without physical contact. Project 7: Hydralic Crane Lift

Participants: Priyanshi Chaterjee (8A), Somdatta Bandopadhya (8A), Pritha Bhattacharya (8A)

Description: This project demonstrates the working of hydraulic cranes, which operate on Pascal's Law. According to this principle, pressure applied to a confined fluid is transmitted equally in all directions, allowing small forces to generate larger lifting forces. By applying pressure within a closed system, the crane can lift heavy loads efficiently. The model incorporates components such as pulleys, wheels, and axles to illustrate how hydraulic systems amplify force, making them essential in real-world construction and material-handling applications.

Project 10: Weather Monitoring System

Participants: Anushka Saha (9A), Ashita Agarwal (9A), Mehul Chakraborty (9A), Antariksha Biswas (9A), Aanshika Patel (9A)

Description: The working principle of a weather forecast is based on measuring and analyzing atmospheric conditions such as temperature, air pressure, humidity, and wind. These factors are recorded using sensors or instruments, and their variations help predict weather changes. For example, falling air pressure and high humidity may indicate rain, while rising pressure suggests clear skies. This model applies these concepts to simulate real weather forecasting in a simplified way.

Project 11: RFID Door Access Control System

Participants: Divyanshu Singh (9A), Tanish Kher (9A), Bihan Sarkar (9A), Neevvedit Jaiswal (9A)

Description: Each RFID card has a unique ID that is recognized by the RFID card reader, making it ideal for secure applications such as hotel room smart lock systems. When a registered card is scanned, the system verifies the ID and activates a servo motor to unlock the door. For added security, the system also includes a secondary unlocking method using a numeric keypad, ensuring dual-layer authentication. The setup consists of jumper wires, an RFID card reader (RC-522), keypad, RFID cards and tags, and servo motors, all integrated to provide a reliable and user-friendly access control solution.

Project 21: Hand Gesture Tank

Participants: Amiyangshu Dutta (11 Comp.Sc), Aryan Roy (11 Comp.Sc), Nakshtra Dutta (11 Comp.Sc), Fahim Azim (11 Comp.Sc)

Description: The Hand Gesture Tank is a robot that operates using an accelerometer to detect and record hand movements. These movements are processed by a comparator, which assigns suitable voltage levels corresponding to the gestures. The processed signals are then passed to an encoder, preparing them for RF transmission. The encoded data is wirelessly sent to the tank, where it is decoded and used to control its motion. This system allows the tank to be maneuvered intuitively using simple hand gestures, making it an innovative and interactive robotics project.

Project 24: PULL IT – Block-and-Tackle Pulley System

Participants: Swastik Kundu (10A), Debaditya Mondal (10A), Divyadyuti Roy (10A), Mrinmoy Rakshit (10A), Baidik Dutta (10A), Swapna Deep Paul (10A)

Description: The PULL IT – Block-and-Tackle Pulley System demonstrates the principle of mechanical advantage using a combination of 3 fixed pulleys and 2 movable pulleys. This arrangement provides a mechanical advantage of 5, meaning it can lift a load five times greater than the effort applied. By distributing the weight across multiple ropes, the system reduces the required effort while making lifting more efficient. This project highlights the practical application of simple machines in reducing human effort and showcases how pulleys are widely used in construction, transport, and everyday lifting mechanisms.

Project 25: Arachnus

Project 27: Central Maximum: Spotlight on Single Slit Diffraction

Participants: Rishi Kumar Datta (12 Sc), Aryan Singh (12 Sc), Akash Jaiswal (12 Sc)

Description: The Spotlight on Single Slit Diffraction project demonstrates the visualization of a diffraction pattern formed when a monochromatic laser source passes through a narrow single slit. The light waves spread out and interfere with one another, producing a distinct pattern of bright and dark fringes on a screen. At the center lies the central maximum, which is the brightest and widest fringe, surrounded by alternating minima and secondary maxima. This experiment highlights the wave nature of light and provides a clear understanding of fundamental optical phenomena.

Women in STEAM: Powering the Future with Robotics & AI

From labs to space, from codes to canvases — women have been at the heart of every leap in STEAM. EXOERGIC 2025 celebrates their courage, brilliance, and groundbreaking achievements, honoring their unstoppable drive to inspire tomorrow’s innovators and leaders.

Global Women Achievers

Meet some extraordinary women who broke barriers, challenged norms, and left a lasting mark on the world of STEAM:

Marie Curie was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize and the only person ever to win Nobel Prizes in two sciences. She discovered polonium and radium, pioneering research that transformed physics and medicine.

Lise Meitner was a physicist who co-discovered nuclear fission in 1939. Despite facing discrimination, she made groundbreaking contributions and is remembered as the “mother of nuclear fission.”

Sofia Kovalevskaya was the first woman professor of mathematics in Northern Europe. She advanced differential equations and mathematical physics, breaking barriers for women in academia.

Grace Hopper, U.S. Navy Rear Admiral, created the first compiler in 1952 and helped develop COBOL. “Amazing Grace” pioneered modern programming.

Shakuntala Devi, the “Human Computer,” stunned the world with extraordinary mental calculations, setting records in 1977 and 1982. She inspired generations with her books and talent.

Karen Spärck Jones introduced Inverse Document Frequency (IDF) in 1972, the foundation of modern search engines. Her work shaped internet search and information retrieval.

Fumiko Yonezawa advanced condensed matter theory and amorphous semiconductors. In 1996, she became the first woman president of the Physical Society of Japan.

Fei-Fei Li co-created ImageNet in 2007, revolutionizing AI and machine learning. She is a Stanford professor and advocate for ethical AI in healthcare.

Timnit Gebru is a leading AI researcher on algorithmic bias and ethics. She co-founded Black in AI and advocates for responsible AI development.

Grazia Deledda, Italian novelist, won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1926. Her works explored women’s struggles, tradition, and modernity.

Hayat Sindi is a pioneering biotechnologist, co-founder of Diagnostics for All, and the first Saudi woman with a Ph.D. in biotechnology from Cambridge. She inspires women in science globally.

Elanor Huntington is an Australian physicist and engineer specializing in quantum computing and systems engineering. A strong advocate for diversity in STEM, she has led major academic institutions.

Pinaree Sanpitak is a Thai contemporary artist exploring femininity, identity, and the female body. She is among Thailand’s most influential artists, blending creativity with global STEAM movements.
